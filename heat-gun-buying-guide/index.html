How to choose the right heat gun

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For most DIYers and homeowners, a dual-temperature heat gun with variable airflow is the sweet spot. Look for a model, like Wagner’s popular range, that offers at least two heat settings and a button or dial to control fan speed. If you need precision for crafts or electronics, a variable-temperature gun with a digital display and lock-on trigger is worth the extra cost.

What to look for in a heat gun

Start with temperature control. The cheapest guns offer a single heat setting and one airflow speed, which works for basic jobs like shrinking tubing. But for paint stripping or softening adhesive, you’ll want at least two heat ranges, typically around 572°F and 1,000°F. so you can dial in the right amount of heat without scorching the workpiece. Airflow is just as important. A gun with variable airflow lets you switch from a gentle breeze for heat-shrink to a strong blast for moving hot air across large surfaces. Look for a lock-on trigger or a constant-switch hold to avoid finger fatigue during long sessions. A built-in stand and a hook for hanging are small but welcome features that keep the hot nozzle off your workbench.

Corded vs cordless heat guns

Corded heat guns deliver consistent, uninterrupted heat and are generally more powerful and affordable. They’re the go-to for extended jobs like stripping multiple layers of paint or heating a large section of PVC. The main drawback is the tether, especially if you’re working on a ladder or in a tight attic. Cordless models, such as those from Milwaukee or DeWalt that run on their respective battery platforms, offer freedom of movement. However, expect trade-offs in runtime and maximum heat output. For occasional use or quick tasks, a cordless gun is convenient. For heavy-duty work, the wall-powered version remains the practical choice. Battery-powered heat guns typically operate at a lower temperature range than their corded cousins.

Temperature control and airflow settings

Variable temperature control is the feature that separates hobbyist tools from professional ones. Basic models use a two-position switch, low and high, while more advanced guns let you adjust in increments, often with a digital display. If you plan to work with heat-shrink tubing, electronics repair, or delicate plastics, a variable-temperature gun prevents melting or burning. Airflow settings range from a gentle 4–5 CFM to a powerful 15–20 CFM. For stripping paint, you often want a broad, slower airflow to soften the paint without blowing it off. For shrink wrapping or drying, a faster stream is helpful. Some guns feature a cool-down mode that runs the fan after the heater shuts off, extending the life of the heating element.

Safety features you shouldn’t overlook

A heat gun gets dangerously hot, nozzle temperatures can exceed 1,000°F. Look for a model that includes a stand that lets you set the gun nozzle-up or on its side, keeping the hot end away from flammable surfaces. An overheat protection circuit that automatically shuts off the gun prevents fire if you set it down absentmindedly. A lock-on trigger is a plus for continuous use, but make sure the lock is easy to disengage in an emergency. Some guns have a silicone grip or a heat shield near the nozzle to protect your hands when working in tight spots. Always choose a gun with a grounded plug (three-prong) if it’s corded; proper grounding reduces the risk of electric shock if the cord gets damaged.

Common uses and tips for beginners

Heat guns excel at removing paint, apply heat until the paint bubbles, then scrape with a putty knife. They also shrink heat-shrink tubing for electrical connections, soften PVC for bending, thaw frozen pipes, loosen rusted bolts, and even dry wet electronics (on low heat). For crafts, they’re great for embossing powders, shrink plastic, and bending acrylic. A few golden rules: Keep the gun moving to avoid scorching. Start on a low heat setting and increase as needed. Never point the nozzle at yourself or pets. Work in a well-ventilated area because many paints and adhesives release toxic fumes when heated. And always set the gun down on its stand when not in use, never on its side or with the nozzle touching a surface.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a heat gun instead of a hair dryer?

Not safely. Heat guns produce far higher temperatures (often 500°F–1,000°F) and can easily burn skin, melt synthetic fibers, or ignite paper. Hair dryers max out around 140°F. Use a heat gun only for its intended industrial or DIY purposes.

What is the best heat gun for stripping paint?

For paint stripping, prioritise a corded gun with a broad, flat nozzle attachment, variable temperature (around 750°F–1,000°F), and adjustable airflow. The Wagner Furno 500 and the DeWalt D26950 are workhorses that owner reviews consistently praise for this task.

Are cordless heat guns powerful enough for most jobs?

Cordless heat guns are fine for light tasks like shrinking tubing, loosening labels, or small electronics work. For sustained, high-heat jobs like stripping multiple layers of paint or bending thick acrylic, a corded model is still far more reliable and powerful.

How do I avoid burning the paint or wood when using a heat gun?

Keep the nozzle several inches away from the surface and constantly moving in a sweeping motion. Use the lowest heat setting that still gets the paint bubbling. Test on a hidden area first. If wood starts to darken or smoke, you’re holding the gun too long in one spot, back off and use a scraper gently.

What attachments should come with a heat gun?

A basic set includes a concentrator nozzle for tight focus on heat-shrink or solder, a flat spreader nozzle for paint stripping, and a glass-protecting nozzle that directs heat around a window pane. Many mid-range kits include these three, plus sometimes a reflector for pipe work.

Can I use a heat gun to thaw frozen pipes?

Yes, but with caution. Use a low heat setting and start at the faucet end, working toward the frozen section. Keep the gun moving and never concentrate heat in one spot, which can burst the pipe. Never use a heat gun near flammable insulation or in a wet area without a GFCI outlet.

In shortA good heat gun is one of the most versatile tools in your workshop, it’ll strip paint, soften plastic, shrink tube, and save you time on dozens of small repairs. Focus on variable temperature and airflow, choose corded for power or cordless for portability, and always respect the heat. With the right model and a bit of practice, that nozzle becomes a precise thermal tool rather than a dangerous blowtorch.
